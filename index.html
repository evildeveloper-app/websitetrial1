Why track at all?

Money stress has a price. Seeing clearly removes it.

The research is consistent: it is not being rich that makes people calm about money. It is knowing exactly where they stand. Three findings shaped how Lucid works.

The distraction tax

Vanguard found workers without an emergency buffer spend over six hours a week distracted by money at work. Four times more than colleagues with just $2,000 set aside. Lucid's home screen answers "where do I stand?" in one glance, so the question stops following you around.

The subscription leak

The average person pays around $90 a month in subscriptions and wastes nearly $200 a year on ones they never use. Lucid's recurring-bills view lists every repeating charge in one place, so nothing quietly drains your account for months unnoticed.

The $2,000 tipping point

A cushion of just $2,000 lifts measured financial well-being by 21 percent. It is the single cheapest happiness upgrade in personal finance. Lucid's savings goals track your progress toward it and celebrate when you arrive.

1 Vanguard emergency-savings research. 2 CNET / CNBC reporting on unused subscriptions. Figures are averages from published studies, not guarantees.
